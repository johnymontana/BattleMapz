This project explores the relationship between power (rosources at the disposal of a country) and fatalities suffered during inter-state militarized disputes. All data comes from the Correlates of War Project datasets.


Some justification for hypothesis. Better military technology (drones, missles, etc). More incentive / access to pursue diplomatic means of mediation that battle, protect infrasstructure and political power before going to war.

Hypothesis

Countries with increasing levels of resources experience decreasing levels of fatalities per war.

Why this is a bad visual

This visual seems to have a good map projection and dataset, but does a poor job of implementation. The legend gives no indication of the units used for population. At first, I thought the units were million of people, however after some quick scrutiny that is clearly not the case. The use of circle overlays in itself is not bad, however the way this visual has moved the overlays aside to allow labeling is problematic. If the intent is to show population density, then keeping the circle overlays positioned geographically correctly is key. Moving them aside only distorts the data being displayed, while not adding much value to the labeling. Instead, the circles should have been kept centered over the city they represent with labeling done inside the circle when needed. This would have more clearly shown population density per geographic region.

Fatality heatmap

This visual uses a heatmap overlay to highlight battle related fatality levels throughout the world. The value of each pixel displayed on the map is defined by its distance to a point that holds data (in this case fatality level for a dispute): \(v=\sum{f_{i} - s*d^w}\) where \(v\) = value of the pixel, \(f_i\) = fatality level for observation \(i\), \(d\) = distance from observation \(i\), \(s\)=0.1 , and \(w\) = 3. The color of the pixel is then determined from the value \(v\) by a color scheme function.
